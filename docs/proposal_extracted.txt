Background Communities in mountainous areas such as Kadugannawa in the Kandy district have become increasingly concerned about the continued increase in traffic accidents in recent years. These regions present a different set of challenges than flat urban road networks, such as narrow, winding roads, sudden elevation changes, and weather that can shift from clear skies to thick fog or torrential rain in a matter of minutes. Drivers of buses, motorcycles, and large trucks handle these conditions in different ways, and when collisions do occur, the results are often dangerous. Although local authorities and residents continue to use them, it is becoming increasingly clear that traditional warning signs and cautions are not enough to prevent lifethreatening accidents. 
This fact makes a more intelligent and flexible approach to driver safety essential. To help drivers avoid dangerous situations before they escalate, the current project focuses on real-time data and predictive modeling in the field of Intelligent Transport Systems (ITS). This is especially important in situations where traditional warning systems cannot distinguish between a long-haul truck approaching a tight curve and a three-wheeler traveling on a steep hill. In complex terrain such as Kadugannawa, most current systems fail to convey the true level of danger as they treat every road and vehicle equally. 
Previous studies [1], [2] have investigated the use of machine learning and big data to predict accident probability. However, these models have mostly focused on urban or highway environments and were created using static information. Although useful, they do not take into account the specific set of conditions that exist in rural, mountainous areas, where weather, road layout, and vehicle behavior combine in an irregular manner. More significantly, no current methodology generates timely, customized warnings by combining real-time data from a car background with past accident trends. 
This discrepancy highlights the core intent of the current investigation. The goal is to develop a large-scale, data-driven warning system that can detect certain hazards for different vehicle types operating on winding and sloping terrain. The project will use machine learning models to create actionable, real-time warnings by combining data from multiple sources, including traffic flow, road gradient information, live weather updates, and previous accident reports. By doing this, the system hopes to proactively assist drivers in avoiding accidents and predict where they are most likely to occur. Ultimately, our strategy goes beyond broad safety recommendations to a smart, context-aware solution that can significantly reduce traffic accidents in difficult situations. 
  
 
 

Literature Review 1. General Landscape of Accident Prediction Research 
Most accident prediction systems have historically been designed and evaluated in flat highway networks or metropolitan settings [3]. These are regions with dense communication networks, better data collection, and more predictable vehicle movement. As a result, a variety of machine learning (ML) and deep learning (DL) models, including CNN, LSTM, SVM, and more recently graph-based models, have been trained on urban datasets [3], with minimal consideration for challenging terrain, including narrow mountain paths, slopes, and sudden bends [4]. 
This research examination makes it clear that urban-based solutions cannot easily be adopted for mountain roads. Most traditional models are unable to identify the specific accident variables introduced by mountain conditions, including elevation changes, blind bends, sudden weather shifts, and vehicle stability issues [4]. 
 
2. Early Approaches (2010-2020): Foundations but Limited Applicability 
Researchers have largely focused on statistical models and traditional machine learning methods over the past decade [5]. These systems have relied primarily on structured factors such as time, traffic flow, driver demographics, and basic environmental details, as well as previous accident statistics [6]. 
For example, 
* since 2010, Bayesian terrain-based techniques have attempted to predict human behavior in complex settings, providing insights into how terrain affects movement. However, these have been primarily designed for general risk assessment or search and rescue situations rather than real-time driving situations [7]. 
* To classify incidents or identify contributing causes, data mining and classification approaches (such as J48, BayesNet, and rough sets) have been used [5]. Although they provide useful pattern recognition, they have had problems with a variety of situations and lacked real-time flexibility.Another limitation of this early stage is that vehicle types were treated as uniform. A bus climbing a steep curve behaves very differently from a motorcycle or a small car, but these models did not account for such differences [8]. 
Despite their shortcomings, these early investigations laid a crucial foundation and emphasized the importance of human behavior, the environment, and data-driven analysis [9]. 
They failed to achieve the goal of creating systems that dynamically respond to real-time risk indicators, especially in remote or geographically difficult locations [10]. 
 
3. Growth of ML/DL and Behavioral Modeling (2021-2023) 
Deep learning, sensor fusion, and graph-based networks were introduced in the later wave of research [11]. The goal of these systems was to better represent the complex relationships between cars, roads, and human drivers [12]. 
 
3.1 IoV Uses and Advanced Sensors 
• OBD-II data to detect aggressive or dangerous behavior [13]. 
• edge computing nodes to process driving data in real time [14]. 
• graph-based neural networks to model spatial and temporal traffic relationships were among the first studies [11]. 
• smartphone sensors to monitor patterns such as swaying or sudden braking [15]. 
Accuracy rates above 90% were observed by several models, especially for accident risk classification and behavior recognition [11]. For example, in supervised settings, graph convergent LSTMs (GConvLSTM) produced highly reliable results [11]. 
3.2 Remaining Challenges 
As accuracy increased, additional issues emerged: 
* Generalizing results was challenging due to proprietary or vendor-specific sensor data. 
* Many datasets were biased toward cities and did not accurately reflect rural or mountainous roads [13]. 
* Much of the research used simulation-based validation, which does not account for the unpredictability of real-world driving [14]. 
* Road gradients, loose dirt, and variations in visibility are examples of terrain-specific accident issues that are still largely ignored [4].  
 
4. Latest Trends (2024-2025): Moving Toward Real-Time Systems, but Still Incomplete 
Embedded systems, IoT-based warning systems, and low-cost versions using microcontrollers and multi-sensor arrays have all received increasing attention in recent years [16] [17]. The aim of these experiments is to develop useful systems that can detect or warn drivers of potential collisions in real time. 
* Drone-assisted search and rescue [18]. 
* CNN-based face analysis for drowsiness detection [19]. 
* GPS-accelerometer-based collision detection [20]. 
* Multi-threshold algorithms for false alarm reduction [21]. 
* Traffic jam and infrastructure-based accident detection are some of the key developments [22]. 
These studies acknowledge real-world challenges more explicitly than earlier work, such as false positives on rough roads, limited network coverage in rural areas, and hardware constraints [23]. Systems designed specifically for mountainous roads have also begun to appear in conceptual form, but many lack prototype-level testing and still rely on simplified assumptions [4]. 
4.1 Persistent Gaps 
Despite these advances, a number of issues remain unresolved. 
* Most real-time systems rely on reliable internet or GPS, which is not always available in remote areas [10]. 
* Mountain road factors such as slopes, sudden elevation changes, and weather interactions are underrepresented [4]. 
* Vehicle-specific risk modeling is still rare. Therefore, while contemporary technologies are largely reliable, they [12]. 
5. Cross-Study Synthesis: What the Literature Agrees On 
Across all phases of the literature, several consistent themes appear: 
5.1 Strengths of Existing Work 
* ML and DL models are excellent at identifying patterns [11]. 
* Complex spatiotemporal relationships are captured by LSTMs and graph networks [11]. ? Low-cost behavioural monitoring is made possible by smartphone and car sensors [15]. 
* Even in environments with limited resources, real-time warnings can be enabled via embedded systems [17]. 
 
5.2 Common Limitations 
* Generalization is hampered by heavy reliance on urban datasets [11]. 
* Warning accuracy is reduced in the absence of vehicle-specific differentiation [12]. 
* Validation is poor in rural or mountainous areas [4]. 
* Most systems ignore environmental factors including rain, fog, elevation, and road structure; real-time solutions often rely on fixed connectivity, which is unsuitable in mountainous locations [4]. 
 Although current research in accident prediction has made progress, the literature consistently highlights several gaps that limit the real-world effectiveness of current systems. Most methods offer limited flexibility for different vehicle types or environments with poor network access, because they only use historical data or are difficult to operate in changing conditions [10]. Moreover, terrain-specific hazards such as steep gradients and sharp curves, which are common in mountainous areas, are rarely taken into account by traditional models. These shortcomings highlight the need for a more adaptable and useful solution that combines historical and current data, and is affordable and reliable in remote environments [4]. Therefore, where current systems are weak, a hybrid accident prediction and warning system based on random forests that incorporates sensor inputs, environmental parameters, and topography information offers a powerful and timely way to improve road safety [12]. 
 
Gaps in Existing Research - Detailed Study 1. No vehicle-specific accident prediction 
Different vehicles (cars, buses, lorries, motorcycles) behave differently on mountain roads, but most research treats all vehicles the same. There are no models designed for each vehicle type. 
2. Mountain-road conditions are not included 
Most studies ignore important mountain-road factors like slope, sharp curves, elevation, and low visibility. These are major reasons for accidents in areas like Kadugannawa. 
3. No complete real-time warning system for hill roads 
Research mainly focuses on highways or city roads. There is no full system that predicts accidents and gives warnings specifically for dangerous mountain roads. 
4. Historical data and live data are not combined 
Studies either use past accident data or real-time data, but not both together. A strong system should combine: 
* past accident trends 
* live weather 
* real-time traffic 
* simple vehicle signals 
  
 
 
6. Too much dependence on expensive or private sensors 
Some systems use special sensors or manufacturer-specific data those normal vehicles don't have. This makes them impractical for mixed vehicles in Sri Lanka. 
 
 
Problem Statement and Research Questions  Problem Statement 
 
"How to develop an accurate ML algorithm, which could early predict the road accidents in mountainous areas based on vehicle type?" This question arises because mountainous roads experience unique hazards such as steep slopes, sharp curves, and sudden weather changes, and different vehicle types cars, motorcycles, buses, and lorries respond to these conditions in different ways. However, existing accident prediction systems are mostly designed for urban or highway environments, treat all vehicles the same, and rarely combine historical data with realtime environmental factors. As a result, there is currently no accurate, vehicle-specific, and terrain-aware accident prediction solution for mountainous regions, creating an urgent need for a machine learning model that can provide early and reliable warnings to reduce accidents in these high-risk areas. 
 
Research Questions 
 
Main Research Question: How to develop an accurate ML algorithm, which could early predict the road accidents in mountainous areas based on vehicle type? 
  RQ1: What existing machine learning algorithms have been used for early prediction of road accidents? 
  RQ2: What are the key road-safety factors in mountainous areas, and which vehicle-specific factors (for cars and motorbikes) influence early accident prediction? 
  RQ3: How can an accurate machine learning algorithm be developed to identify road accidents in advance within mountainous environments? 
  RQ4: How can the algorithm developed in RQ3 be evaluated to ensure it predicts road accidents early enough to support safer driving on mountainous roads?
